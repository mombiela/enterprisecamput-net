Introduction

Orthogonal projections are a fundamental tool in 3D mathematics and have applications in various areas such as engineering, architecture, and computer graphics. In this topic, we will learn what orthogonal projections are, how they are calculated, and how they are applied in different contexts.

Key Concepts

  • Orthogonal Projection: It is the representation of a point or an object on a plane or line, such that the line connecting the original point with its projection is perpendicular to the projection plane or line.
  • Projection Plane: It is the plane onto which the object is projected.
  • Normal Vector: It is a vector perpendicular to the projection plane.

Projection of a Point onto a Line

To project a point \( P \) onto a line \( L \) in space, follow the procedure below:

  1. Identify the point and the line: Suppose the point \( P \) has coordinates \( (x_1, y_1, z_1) \) and the line \( L \) is defined by a point \( A \) with coordinates \( (x_2, y_2, z_2) \) and a direction vector \( \vec{d} = (a, b, c) \).
  2. Calculate the vector \( \vec{AP} \): This is the vector that goes from point \( A \) to point \( P \), given by \( \vec{AP} = (x_1 - x_2, y_1 - y_2, z_1 - z_2) \).
  3. Scalar projection: The scalar projection of \( \vec{AP} \) onto \( \vec{d} \) is \( \text{proj}_{\vec{d}} \vec{AP} = \frac{\vec{AP} \cdot \vec{d}}{\vec{d} \cdot \vec{d}} \).
  4. Vector projection: The vector projection of \( \vec{AP} \) onto \( \vec{d} \) is \( \text{proj}_{\vec{d}} \vec{AP} = \left( \frac{\vec{AP} \cdot \vec{d}}{\vec{d} \cdot \vec{d}} \right) \vec{d} \).
  5. Projected point: The projected point \( P' \) is obtained by adding point \( A \) and the vector projection: \( P' = A + \text{proj}_{\vec{d}} \vec{AP} \).

Example

Suppose we have the point \( P(3, 4, 5) \) and the line \( L \) defined by the point \( A(1, 2, 3) \) and the direction vector \( \vec{d} = (1, 1, 1) \).

  1. Vector \( \vec{AP} \): \[ \vec{AP} = (3 - 1, 4 - 2, 5 - 3) = (2, 2, 2) \]

  2. Scalar projection: \[ \text{proj}_{\vec{d}} \vec{AP} = \frac{(2, 2, 2) \cdot (1, 1, 1)}{(1, 1, 1) \cdot (1, 1, 1)} = \frac{2 + 2 + 2}{1 + 1 + 1} = 2 \]

  3. Vector projection: \[ \text{proj}_{\vec{d}} \vec{AP} = 2 \cdot (1, 1, 1) = (2, 2, 2) \]

  4. Projected point: \[ P' = (1, 2, 3) + (2, 2, 2) = (3, 4, 5) \]

In this case, the projected point \( P' \) coincides with the original point \( P \), indicating that \( P \) was already on the line \( L \).

Projection of a Point onto a Plane

To project a point \( P \) onto a plane \( \Pi \), follow the procedure below:

  1. Identify the point and the plane: Suppose the point \( P \) has coordinates \( (x_1, y_1, z_1) \) and the plane \( \Pi \) is defined by the equation \( ax + by + cz + d = 0 \).
  2. Normal vector: The normal vector to the plane is \( \vec{n} = (a, b, c) \).
  3. Scalar projection: The scalar projection of \( \vec{OP} \) onto \( \vec{n} \) is \( \text{proj}_{\vec{n}} \vec{OP} = \frac{\vec{OP} \cdot \vec{n}}{\vec{n} \cdot \vec{n}} \).
  4. Vector projection: The vector projection of \( \vec{OP} \) onto \( \vec{n} \) is \( \text{proj}_{\vec{n}} \vec{OP} = \left( \frac{\vec{OP} \cdot \vec{n}}{\vec{n} \cdot \vec{n}} \right) \vec{n} \).
  5. Projected point: The projected point \( P' \) is obtained by subtracting the vector projection from the original point: \( P' = P - \text{proj}_{\vec{n}} \vec{OP} \).

Example

Suppose we have the point \( P(3, 4, 5) \) and the plane \( \Pi \) defined by the equation \( x + y + z - 6 = 0 \).

  1. Normal vector: \[ \vec{n} = (1, 1, 1) \]

  2. Scalar projection: \[ \text{proj}_{\vec{n}} \vec{OP} = \frac{(3, 4, 5) \cdot (1, 1, 1)}{(1, 1, 1) \cdot (1, 1, 1)} = \frac{3 + 4 + 5}{1 + 1 + 1} = 4 \]

  3. Vector projection: \[ \text{proj}_{\vec{n}} \vec{OP} = 4 \cdot (1, 1, 1) = (4, 4, 4) \]

  4. Projected point: \[ P' = (3, 4, 5) - (4, 4, 4) = (-1, 0, 1) \]

The projected point \( P' \) is \( (-1, 0, 1) \).

Conclusion

Orthogonal projections are an essential technique in 3D mathematics for representing points and objects on different planes and lines. Understanding how to calculate these projections allows solving complex problems in various disciplines. Practicing these concepts with additional examples and exercises will help consolidate the acquired knowledge.
