Introduction

Cartesian coordinates in 3D are an extension of the Cartesian coordinate system in 2D, allowing the representation of points in a three-dimensional space. This system is fundamental in mathematics, physics, engineering, and many other disciplines. In this topic, we will explore the basic concepts, notation, and some practical examples to better understand how Cartesian coordinates in 3D work.

Basic Concepts

  • Cartesian Coordinate System: A reference system in which each point in space is described by three coordinates (x, y, z).
  • Coordinate Axes: Three mutually perpendicular axes, generally labeled as X, Y, and Z.
    • X-Axis: Represents the horizontal dimension.
    • Y-Axis: Represents the vertical dimension.
    • Z-Axis: Represents depth.
  • Origin: The point (0, 0, 0) where the three axes intersect.

Notation

A point in three-dimensional space is denoted as \( P(x, y, z) \), where:

  • \( x \) is the coordinate on the X-axis.
  • \( y \) is the coordinate on the Y-axis.
  • \( z \) is the coordinate on the Z-axis.

Practical Examples

Example 1: Representation of a Point

Consider the point \( P(3, 4, 5) \). This point is located:

  • 3 units along the X-axis.
  • 4 units along the Y-axis.
  • 5 units along the Z-axis.

Example 2: Distance between Two Points

To calculate the distance between two points \( P1(x1, y1, z1) \) and \( P2(x2, y2, z2) \), we use the Euclidean distance formula in 3D:

\[ d = \sqrt{(x2 - x1)^2 + (y2 - y1)^2 + (z2 - z1)^2} \]

Calculation Example

Find the distance between the points \( P1(1, 2, 3) \) and \( P2(4, 6, 8) \).

\[ d = \sqrt{(4 - 1)^2 + (6 - 2)^2 + (8 - 3)^2} \] \[ d = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2 + 5^2} \] \[ d = \sqrt{9 + 16 + 25} \] \[ d = \sqrt{50} \] \[ d \approx 7.07 \]

Exercises

  1. Find the coordinates of the midpoint:

    • Given the points \( A(2, 3, 4) \) and \( B(6, 7, 8) \), find the coordinates of the midpoint \( M \).

  2. Calculate the distance:

    • Calculate the distance between the points \( P1(1, 1, 1) \) and \( P2(4, 5, 6) \).

  3. Transformations:

    • If the point \( P(2, 3, 4) \) is translated 3 units along the X-axis, -2 units along the Y-axis, and 5 units along the Z-axis, what are the new coordinates of the point?

Conclusion

Cartesian coordinates in 3D are an essential tool for representing and manipulating points in three-dimensional space. Understanding how the axes work, the notation, and basic formulas like the distance between two points is fundamental for advancing in more complex studies in mathematics and applied sciences. Practicing with examples and exercises will help consolidate these concepts and prepare for more advanced applications.
