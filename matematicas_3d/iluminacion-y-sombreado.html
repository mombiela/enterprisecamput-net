Introduction

Lighting and shading are essential components in creating realistic 3D graphics. These concepts allow objects in a 3D scene to interact with light sources in a way that makes them look more natural and three-dimensional. In this section, we will explore the fundamentals of lighting and shading, including the most common lighting models and how they are implemented in 3D graphics.

Lighting Models

Ambient Lighting

  • Description: Represents indirect light that scatters in all directions and affects all objects in the scene equally.
  • Equation: \( I_{\text{ambient}} = k_{\text{ambient}} \cdot I_{\text{light}} \)
    • \( k_{\text{ambient}} \): Ambient lighting coefficient of the material.
    • \( I_{\text{light}} \): Intensity of the ambient light.

Diffuse Lighting

  • Description: Represents light that directly hits a surface and scatters in all directions. It depends on the angle of incidence of the light.
  • Equation: \( I_{\text{diffuse}} = k_{\text{diffuse}} \cdot I_{\text{light}} \cdot (\vec{L} \cdot \vec{N}) \)
    • \( k_{\text{diffuse}} \): Diffuse lighting coefficient of the material.
    • \( \vec{L} \): Light direction vector.
    • \( \vec{N} \): Surface normal vector.

Specular Lighting

  • Description: Represents light that reflects off a surface in a specific direction, creating bright spots known as "highlights".
  • Equation: \( I_{\text{specular}} = k_{\text{specular}} \cdot I_{\text{light}} \cdot (\vec{R} \cdot \vec{V})^n \)
    • \( k_{\text{specular}} \): Specular lighting coefficient of the material.
    • \( \vec{R} \): Light reflection vector.
    • \( \vec{V} \): View vector (towards the observer).
    • \( n \): Shininess exponent (determines the concentration of the highlight).

Shading Models

Flat Shading

  • Description: Applies a single color to each polygon based on the lighting at a specific point (usually the center or a vertex).
  • Advantages: Fast and simple to calculate.
  • Disadvantages: Produces unrealistic results, especially on curved surfaces.

Gouraud Shading

  • Description: Calculates lighting at each vertex and then interpolates the colors across the surface of the polygon.
  • Advantages: More realistic than flat shading, suitable for smooth surfaces.
  • Disadvantages: May not correctly capture specular highlights.

Phong Shading

  • Description: Interpolates vertex normals across the surface of the polygon and calculates lighting at each pixel.
  • Advantages: Produces very realistic results, capturing specular highlights well.
  • Disadvantages: More computationally expensive than Gouraud shading.

Code Examples

Diffuse Lighting in GLSL

#version 330 core

in vec3 Normal;
in vec3 LightDir;

uniform vec3 LightColor;
uniform vec3 ObjectColor;

out vec4 FragColor;

void main()
{
    // Normalize vectors
    vec3 norm = normalize(Normal);
    vec3 lightDir = normalize(LightDir);
    
    // Calculate diffuse component
    float diff = max(dot(norm, lightDir), 0.0);
    vec3 diffuse = diff * LightColor;
    
    // Calculate final color
    vec3 result = (diffuse + ambient) * ObjectColor;
    FragColor = vec4(result, 1.0);
}

Phong Shading in GLSL

#version 330 core

in vec3 FragPos;
in vec3 Normal;

uniform vec3 LightPos;
uniform vec3 ViewPos;
uniform vec3 LightColor;
uniform vec3 ObjectColor;

out vec4 FragColor;

void main()
{
    // Normalize vectors
    vec3 norm = normalize(Normal);
    vec3 lightDir = normalize(LightPos - FragPos);
    vec3 viewDir = normalize(ViewPos - FragPos);
    vec3 reflectDir = reflect(-lightDir, norm);
    
    // Diffuse component
    float diff = max(dot(norm, lightDir), 0.0);
    vec3 diffuse = diff * LightColor;
    
    // Specular component
    float spec = pow(max(dot(viewDir, reflectDir), 0.0), 32);
    vec3 specular = spec * LightColor;
    
    // Ambient component
    vec3 ambient = 0.1 * LightColor;
    
    // Calculate final color
    vec3 result = (ambient + diffuse + specular) * ObjectColor;
    FragColor = vec4(result, 1.0);
}

Conclusion

Lighting and shading are fundamental to creating realistic 3D graphics. Understanding the different lighting models (ambient, diffuse, and specular) and shading models (flat, Gouraud, and Phong) is crucial for any professional in the field of 3D graphics. Through code examples, we have seen how to implement these concepts in GLSL, providing a solid foundation for future explorations and applications in 3D graphics.
