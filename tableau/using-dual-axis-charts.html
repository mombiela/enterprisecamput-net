Introduction

Dual-axis charts, also known as combination charts, allow you to compare two different measures in a single visualization. This can be particularly useful for identifying relationships between variables, comparing trends, and enhancing data storytelling.

Key Concepts

  • Dual-Axis Chart: A chart that uses two axes to plot two different measures.
  • Primary Axis: The main axis used for the first measure.
  • Secondary Axis: The secondary axis used for the second measure.
  • Synchronization: Aligning the scales of the primary and secondary axes for better comparison.

Creating a Dual-Axis Chart

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Connect to Data Source: Open Tableau and connect to your data source.
  2. Drag Measures to Rows: Drag the first measure to the Rows shelf.
  3. Drag Measures to Columns: Drag the second measure to the Columns shelf.
  4. Create Dual-Axis: Right-click on the second measure on the Rows shelf and select "Dual-Axis".
  5. Synchronize Axes: Right-click on the secondary axis and select "Synchronize Axis" to align the scales.

Example

Let's create a dual-axis chart to compare Sales and Profit over time.

  1. Connect to Sample Data: Use Tableau's Sample - Superstore dataset.
  2. Drag Order Date to Columns: Drag Order Date to the Columns shelf.
  3. Drag Sales to Rows: Drag Sales to the Rows shelf.
  4. Drag Profit to Rows: Drag Profit to the Rows shelf, next to Sales.
  5. Create Dual-Axis: Right-click on the Profit pill on the Rows shelf and select "Dual-Axis".
  6. Synchronize Axes: Right-click on the secondary axis (Profit) and select "Synchronize Axis".
  7. Customize Marks: Change the mark type for each measure to better visualize the data (e.g., line for Sales and bar for Profit).

Customizing Dual-Axis Charts

Changing Mark Types

  • Line and Bar Combination: Use a line for one measure and bars for another to highlight trends and differences.
  • Shape and Color: Customize shapes and colors to differentiate between the two measures.

Adding Reference Lines

  • Reference Lines: Add reference lines to highlight specific values or trends.
  • Annotations: Use annotations to provide additional context or insights.

Formatting Axes

  • Axis Titles: Customize axis titles to clearly indicate what each axis represents.
  • Tick Marks: Adjust tick marks for better readability.

Best Practices

  • Synchronization: Always synchronize axes to ensure accurate comparison.
  • Clarity: Use clear and distinct colors and shapes to differentiate measures.
  • Context: Provide context with annotations and reference lines to enhance understanding.

Common Use Cases

  • Sales vs. Profit: Compare sales and profit over time to identify trends and correlations.
  • Temperature vs. Precipitation: Compare temperature and precipitation to study weather patterns.
  • Stock Prices vs. Volume: Analyze stock prices and trading volume to understand market behavior.

Conclusion

Dual-axis charts in Tableau are powerful tools for comparing two measures in a single visualization. By following best practices and customizing your charts, you can create compelling and insightful visualizations that enhance your data storytelling. Remember to always synchronize your axes and use clear distinctions between measures to ensure your audience can easily interpret the data.
