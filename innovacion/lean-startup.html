Introduction

The concept of Lean Startup, developed by Eric Ries, is a methodology for developing businesses and products that focuses on shortening product development cycles and continuously measuring progress. This methodology is especially useful in the field of innovation, where uncertainty and risk are high.

Fundamental Principles

  • Build-Measure-Learn: This cycle is the core of the Lean Startup methodology. The idea is to build a minimum viable product (MVP), measure its performance, and learn from the results to iterate and improve the product.
  • Minimum Viable Product (MVP): An MVP is the simplest version of a product that allows for the maximum amount of validated learning with the least effort.
  • Validated Learning: It is the process of empirically demonstrating that a team has discovered fundamental truths about the business prospects of its product.
  • Pivot or Persevere: Based on validated learning, a startup must decide whether to pivot (change direction) or persevere (stay the course).

Build-Measure-Learn Cycle

  1. Build: Create an MVP to test hypotheses.
  2. Measure: Collect key data and metrics to evaluate the MVP's performance.
  3. Learn: Analyze the data to gain insights and make informed decisions.

Example of Build-Measure-Learn Cycle

Suppose we are developing a mobile app for time management.

  1. Build: We create a basic version of the app that allows users to log their daily activities.
  2. Measure: We implement analytics tools to track how many users download the app, how many use it daily, and which features they use the most.
  3. Learn: We discover that users find the reminder feature useful but do not use the time analysis feature. We decide to improve the reminders and reconsider or eliminate the time analysis feature.

Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

The MVP is crucial for validating hypotheses with the least effort and resources possible. It is not about creating an incomplete product but about developing a version that allows for maximum learning with minimal effort.

Example of MVP

If we are developing an e-commerce platform, an MVP could be a simple webpage with a list of products and a purchase button that redirects to a contact form instead of a complete payment system.

Validated Learning

Validated learning is the process of empirically demonstrating that something valuable has been learned about customer needs and the market. This is achieved through continuous experiments and tests.

Example of Validated Learning

If we launch a marketing campaign for our MVP and observe that 70% of visitors click the buy button, we can validate that there is interest in our products. However, if only 5% complete the contact form, we learn that we need to improve the purchase process.

Pivot or Persevere

After each Build-Measure-Learn cycle, we must decide whether to pivot (change direction) or persevere (stay the current course).

  • Pivot: Change some fundamental aspect of the product or strategy based on validated learning.
  • Persevere: Continue with the same approach if the results are positive.

Example of Pivot

If we discover that users are more interested in a secondary feature of our app, we could pivot to focus on developing that feature as the main one.

Conclusion

The Lean Startup methodology offers a structured and scientific approach to innovation and product development. By focusing on rapid cycles of building, measuring, and learning, startups can minimize risk and maximize learning, allowing for quick adaptation to market needs and increasing the chances of success.

By applying these principles, IT professionals can develop more efficient products aligned with real user needs, optimizing resources and time in the process.
