Introduction to Normalization

Normalization is a fundamental process in the design of relational databases. Its main objective is to organize data to reduce redundancy and improve data integrity. Through normalization, large tables are broken down into smaller tables and relationships are defined between them, making database management and maintenance easier.

Objectives of Normalization

  • Eliminate data redundancy: Reduce data duplication to save space and avoid inconsistencies.
  • Improve data integrity: Ensure that data is accurate and consistent.
  • Facilitate maintenance: Make the database easier to update and maintain.

Normal Forms

Normalization is carried out in several stages, known as normal forms. Each normal form has a set of rules that must be met for a table to be considered normalized at that level.

First Normal Form (1NF)

For a table to be in the First Normal Form (1NF), it must meet the following criteria:

  • Atomicity: Each column must contain atomic, i.e., indivisible, values.
  • Uniqueness of rows: Each row must be unique.

Example:

Suppose we have a student table with the following data:

| Student_ID | Name | Courses | |------------|---------------|---------------------| | 1 | Juan Pérez | Mathematics, Physics| | 2 | Ana Gómez | Chemistry |

To convert this table to 1NF, we must break down the multiple values into separate columns:

| Student_ID | Name | Course | |------------|---------------|-------------| | 1 | Juan Pérez | Mathematics | | 1 | Juan Pérez | Physics | | 2 | Ana Gómez | Chemistry |

Second Normal Form (2NF)

For a table to be in the Second Normal Form (2NF), it must meet the following criteria:

  • Must be in 1NF.
  • Elimination of partial dependencies: All non-key attributes must fully depend on the primary key.

Example:

Suppose we have an orders table with the following data:

| Order_ID | Customer_ID | Customer_Name | Product | Quantity | |----------|-------------|---------------|-----------|----------| | 1 | 101 | Juan Pérez | Laptop | 1 | | 2 | 102 | Ana Gómez | Smartphone| 2 |

To convert this table to 2NF, we must eliminate partial dependencies:

Customers Table:

| Customer_ID | Customer_Name | |-------------|---------------| | 101 | Juan Pérez | | 102 | Ana Gómez |

Orders Table:

| Order_ID | Customer_ID | Product | Quantity | |----------|-------------|------------|----------| | 1 | 101 | Laptop | 1 | | 2 | 102 | Smartphone | 2 |

Third Normal Form (3NF)

For a table to be in the Third Normal Form (3NF), it must meet the following criteria:

  • Must be in 2NF.
  • Elimination of transitive dependencies: Non-key attributes must depend solely on the primary key.

Example:

Suppose we have an employees table with the following data:

| Employee_ID | Name | Department | Department_Head | |-------------|-------------|------------|-----------------| | 1 | Juan Pérez | IT | Carlos López | | 2 | Ana Gómez | HR | Marta Sánchez |

To convert this table to 3NF, we must eliminate transitive dependencies:

Employees Table:

| Employee_ID | Name | Department_ID | |-------------|------------|---------------| | 1 | Juan Pérez | 1 | | 2 | Ana Gómez | 2 |

Departments Table:

| Department_ID | Department | Department_Head | |---------------|------------|-----------------| | 1 | IT | Carlos López | | 2 | HR | Marta Sánchez |

Conclusion

Normalization is a crucial process in database design that helps reduce redundancy and improve data integrity. Through the different normal forms (1NF, 2NF, 3NF), we can structure our tables efficiently and logically. Understanding and applying these concepts is essential for any IT professional working with relational databases.
