Introduction

In this topic, we will explore the fundamental concepts of planes and lines in three-dimensional space. These concepts are essential for understanding 3D geometry and have applications in various fields such as engineering, physics, and computer science.

Lines in Space

A line in three-dimensional space can be represented in several ways. The two most common forms are the parametric form and the vector form.

Parametric Form

The parametric form of a line is expressed using a parameter \( t \). If a line passes through a point \( P_0(x_0, y_0, z_0) \) and has a direction given by the vector \( \vec{d} = (a, b, c) \), then the parametric equation of the line is:

\[ \begin{cases} x = x_0 + at
y = y_0 + bt
z = z_0 + ct \end{cases} \]

Vector Form

The vector form of a line uses vectors to describe the position of any point on the line. The vector equation of a line that passes through the point \( P_0 \) and has a direction \( \vec{d} \) is:

\[ \vec{r}(t) = \vec{r_0} + t\vec{d} \]

where \( \vec{r_0} \) is the position vector of the point \( P_0 \) and \( t \) is a real parameter.

Example

Consider a line that passes through the point \( P_0(1, 2, 3) \) and has a direction given by the vector \( \vec{d} = (4, -2, 1) \).

  • Parametric Form:

\[ \begin{cases} x = 1 + 4t
y = 2 - 2t
z = 3 + t \end{cases} \]

  • Vector Form:

\[ \vec{r}(t) = (1, 2, 3) + t(4, -2, 1) \]

Planes in Space

A plane in three-dimensional space can be represented by a general equation or a vector form.

General Equation of the Plane

The general equation of a plane in space is:

\[ ax + by + cz + d = 0 \]

where \( a \), \( b \), \( c \), and \( d \) are constants and \( (a, b, c) \) is the normal vector to the plane.

Vector Form of the Plane

The vector form of a plane can be expressed using a point on the plane and two direction vectors. If a plane passes through the point \( P_0(x_0, y_0, z_0) \) and has two direction vectors \( \vec{u} \) and \( \vec{v} \), then the vector equation of the plane is:

\[ \vec{r} = \vec{r_0} + s\vec{u} + t\vec{v} \]

where \( \vec{r_0} \) is the position vector of the point \( P_0 \) and \( s \) and \( t \) are real parameters.

Example

Consider a plane that passes through the point \( P_0(1, 2, 3) \) and has a normal vector \( \vec{n} = (2, -1, 3) \).

  • General Equation:

\[ 2x - y + 3z + d = 0 \]

To find \( d \), we substitute the point \( P_0 \):

\[ 2(1) - 1(2) + 3(3) + d = 0 \] \[ 2 - 2 + 9 + d = 0 \] \[ d = -9 \]

Therefore, the equation of the plane is:

\[ 2x - y + 3z - 9 = 0 \]

Intersection of Lines and Planes

The intersection of a line and a plane can be a point, a line (if the line is contained in the plane), or nonexistent (if the line is parallel to the plane).

Example

Consider the line \( \vec{r}(t) = (1, 2, 3) + t(4, -2, 1) \) and the plane \( 2x - y + 3z - 9 = 0 \).

To find the intersection, we substitute the parametric equations of the line into the equation of the plane:

\[ 2(1 + 4t) - (2 - 2t) + 3(3 + t) - 9 = 0 \] \[ 2 + 8t - 2 + 2t + 9 + 3t - 9 = 0 \] \[ 13t = 0 \] \[ t = 0 \]

Substituting \( t = 0 \) into the parametric equations of the line, we obtain the point of intersection:

\[ (x, y, z) = (1, 2, 3) \]

Conclusion

In this topic, we have explored the representations of lines and planes in three-dimensional space, as well as the intersection between them. Understanding these concepts is fundamental for working with 3D geometry and has practical applications in various disciplines. Be sure to practice with different examples to consolidate your understanding.
