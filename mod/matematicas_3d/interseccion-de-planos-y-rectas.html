Introduction

In the study of three-dimensional geometry, one of the fundamental topics is the intersection of planes and lines. This concept is crucial for various applications in engineering, physics, computer graphics, and more. In this section, we will explore how to determine the intersection between planes and lines, and how to represent these intersections mathematically.

Key Concepts

  • Plane in 3D: A plane in three-dimensional space can be represented by the general equation \(Ax + By + Cz + D = 0\), where \(A\), \(B\), \(C\), and \(D\) are constants.
  • Line in 3D: A line in three-dimensional space can be represented parametrically as: \[ \begin{cases} x = x_0 + at
    y = y_0 + bt
    z = z_0 + ct \end{cases} \] where \((x_0, y_0, z_0)\) is a point on the line, \((a, b, c)\) is the direction vector of the line, and \(t\) is a parameter.

Intersection of a Line and a Plane

To find the intersection of a line and a plane, follow this procedure:

  1. Parametric Substitution: Substitute the parametric equations of the line into the plane equation.
  2. Solving for the Parameter: Solve the resulting equation for the parameter \(t\).
  3. Determining the Intersection Point: Substitute the value of \(t\) into the parametric equations of the line to find the intersection point.

Example

Consider the line given by: \[ \begin{cases} x = 1 + 2t
y = -1 + 3t
z = 4 - t \end{cases} \]

and the plane given by: \[2x - y + z = 3\]

Step 1: Parametric Substitution

Substitute \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) from the line into the plane equation: \[2(1 + 2t) - (-1 + 3t) + (4 - t) = 3\]

Step 2: Solving for the Parameter

Simplify the equation: \[2 + 4t + 1 - 3t + 4 - t = 3\] \[7 = 3\]

This indicates that the equation is inconsistent, meaning the line and the plane do not intersect.

Intersection of Two Planes

To find the intersection of two planes, follow this procedure:

  1. System of Equations: Solve the system of equations formed by the equations of the two planes.
  2. Determining the Line of Intersection: The solution to the system of equations will be a line, which can be represented parametrically.

Example

Consider the planes: \[P_1: x + y + z = 1\] \[P_2: 2x - y + 3z = 4\]

Step 1: System of Equations

Solve the system: \[ \begin{cases} x + y + z = 1
2x - y + 3z = 4 \end{cases} \]

Step 2: Solving the System

Add the two equations to eliminate \(y\): \[3x + 4z = 5\] \[x = \frac{5 - 4z}{3}\]

Substitute \(x\) into the first equation: \[\frac{5 - 4z}{3} + y + z = 1\] \[y = 1 - z - \frac{5 - 4z}{3}\] \[y = \frac{-2 + z}{3}\]

The line of intersection can be written parametrically as: \[ \begin{cases} x = \frac{5 - 4t}{3}
y = \frac{-2 + t}{3}
z = t \end{cases} \]

Conclusion

The intersection of planes and lines is a fundamental topic in three-dimensional geometry. Knowing how to find these points and lines of intersection is crucial for solving complex problems in various disciplines. Through examples and exercises, we have demonstrated how to approach these problems systematically and effectively.
