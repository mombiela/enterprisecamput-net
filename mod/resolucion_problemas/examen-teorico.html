Introduction

The theoretical exam is a fundamental tool for evaluating the understanding and application of key concepts in problem-solving. This exam focuses on measuring participants' ability to identify, analyze, and propose effective solutions to various types of problems in work environments.

Exam Objectives

  • Evaluate the understanding of theoretical concepts of problem-solving.
  • Measure the ability to apply problem-solving techniques and tools in hypothetical situations.
  • Identify areas for improvement in participants' knowledge and skills.

Exam Structure

The theoretical exam is divided into several sections, each designed to evaluate different aspects of problem-solving:

  1. Fundamental Concepts

    • Definition of a problem.
    • Types of problems (structured vs. unstructured).
    • Stages of the problem-solving process.

  2. Techniques and Tools

    • Root cause analysis.
    • Ishikawa diagram (fishbone diagram).
    • Decision matrix.
    • SWOT analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats).

  3. Practical Application

    • Case studies.
    • Multiple-choice questions based on scenarios.
    • Open-ended questions for solution formulation.

Sample Questions

Fundamental Concepts

  • Question 1: What is the difference between a structured problem and an unstructured problem?

    • Answer: A structured problem has a clear and defined solution, while an unstructured problem does not have an obvious solution and may require a more creative and flexible approach.

  • Question 2: List the stages of the problem-solving process.

    • Answer: Problem identification, problem analysis, generation of alternatives, evaluation of alternatives, solution implementation, and follow-up and evaluation.

Techniques and Tools

  • Question 3: Explain how an Ishikawa diagram is used to identify the root cause of a problem.

    • Answer: The Ishikawa diagram, also known as the fishbone diagram, is used to identify potential causes of a problem by organizing them into main categories such as people, processes, materials, and environment. Each category is broken down into sub-causes until the root cause is reached.

  • Question 4: What is a SWOT analysis and how is it applied in problem-solving?

    • Answer: A SWOT analysis is a tool that evaluates the Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats of a situation or problem. It is used to identify internal and external factors that can influence problem-solving and to develop effective strategies.

Practical Application

  • Question 5: In a case study where a company faces a decline in sales, what steps would you take to identify and solve the problem?
    • Answer: First, I would identify the specific problem (decline in sales). Then, I would analyze the possible causes (marketing issues, competition, product quality). I would generate alternatives (new marketing strategies, product improvements). I would evaluate the alternatives and select the best option. I would implement the solution and follow up to assess its effectiveness.

Conclusion

The theoretical exam is a crucial part of the problem-solving course, as it allows for the evaluation of the understanding and application of the concepts learned. Through questions that range from basic theory to practical application, participants can demonstrate their ability to effectively address and solve problems in their work environments. This exam not only measures the knowledge acquired but also identifies areas for improvement for the continuous development of problem-solving skills.
