Introduction to ER Diagrams

Entity-Relationship (ER) Diagrams are a fundamental tool in database design. They allow visualizing the logical structure of a database graphically, facilitating its understanding and design.

  • Entity: Represents a real-world object or concept that has an independent existence. Examples: Customer, Product, Order.
  • Attribute: Property or characteristic of an entity. Examples: name, price, date.
  • Relationship: Association between two or more entities. Examples: A customer places an order, a product belongs to a category.

Components of an ER Diagram

Entities

Entities are represented by rectangles and can be of two types:

  • Strong entities: Have an independent existence. Example: Customer.
  • Weak entities: Depend on another entity for their existence. Example: Order Detail.

Attributes

Attributes are represented by ovals and are connected to their corresponding entity by a line.

  • Simple attributes: Cannot be divided into subparts. Example: Phone number.
  • Composite attributes: Can be divided into subparts. Example: Address (Street, City, Postal Code).
  • Multivalued attributes: Can have multiple values. Example: Contact phones.
  • Derived attributes: Can be calculated from other attributes. Example: Age (from birth date).

Relationships

Relationships are represented by diamonds and connect two or more entities.

  • Binary relationships: Involve two entities. Example: A customer places an order.
  • Ternary relationships: Involve three entities. Example: A supplier provides products to a store.

Cardinality

Cardinality defines the number of instances of one entity that can be associated with an instance of another entity.

  • One to one (1:1): One instance of one entity is associated with one instance of another entity.
  • One to many (1:N): One instance of one entity is associated with multiple instances of another entity.
  • Many to many (M:N): Multiple instances of one entity are associated with multiple instances of another entity.

Example of an ER Diagram

Below is an example of an ER diagram for an online store database:

[Customer]---(Places)---[Order]---(Contains)---[Product]
   |                        |                        |
[Customer_ID]              [Order_ID]             [Product_ID]
[Name]                     [Date]                 [Name]
[Email]                    [Total]                [Price]

In this example:

  • Entities: Customer, Order, Product.
  • Relationships: Places, Contains.
  • Attributes: Customer_ID, Name, Email, Order_ID, Date, Total, Product_ID, Price.

Practical Exercise

Design an ER diagram for a library database. Consider the following entities and attributes:

  • Book: ISBN, Title, Author, Year of Publication.
  • User: User_ID, Name, Address, Phone.
  • Loan: Loan_ID, Loan Date, Return Date.

Define the relationships between these entities and their cardinality.

Conclusion

Entity-Relationship Diagrams are an essential tool for database design. They allow representing entities, attributes, and relationships clearly and concisely, facilitating the understanding and development of the logical structure of the database. Practicing ER diagram design is fundamental to acquiring solid skills in database modeling.
