Introduction to Normalization

Normalization is a process used in database design to minimize redundancy and avoid inconsistency issues. This process is carried out by organizing data into tables and applying certain rules known as normal forms.

Key Concepts

  • Normal Form (NF): A set of rules applied to database tables to ensure that data is organized efficiently.
  • Functional Dependency: A relationship between two sets of attributes in a table, where one set of attributes determines another set.
  • Primary Key: An attribute or set of attributes that uniquely identifies a row in a table.
  • Foreign Key: An attribute in a table that is a primary key in another table and is used to establish relationships between tables.

Normal Forms

First Normal Form (1NF)

For a table to be in 1NF, it must meet the following requirements:

  • The values of each column must be atomic (indivisible).
  • Each column must contain values of the same type.
  • Each column must have a unique name.
  • The order in which data is stored should not matter.

Example:

Suppose we have the following table of students and their courses:

StudentID Name Courses
1 Alice Math, Science
2 Bob Math, English
3 Charlie Science, English

To convert this table to 1NF, we must split the courses into separate rows:

StudentID Name Course
1 Alice Math
1 Alice Science
2 Bob Math
2 Bob English
3 Charlie Science
3 Charlie English

Second Normal Form (2NF)

For a table to be in 2NF, it must meet the requirements of 1NF and also:

  • All non-key attributes must depend entirely on the primary key.

Example:

Suppose we have the following table in 1NF:

StudentID Course Instructor
1 Math Dr. Smith
1 Science Dr. Brown
2 Math Dr. Smith
2 English Dr. White
3 Science Dr. Brown
3 English Dr. White

To convert this table to 2NF, we must eliminate partial dependencies. We can create two tables:

Students and Courses Table:

StudentID Course
1 Math
1 Science
2 Math
2 English
3 Science
3 English

Courses and Instructors Table:

Course Instructor
Math Dr. Smith
Science Dr. Brown
English Dr. White

Third Normal Form (3NF)

For a table to be in 3NF, it must meet the requirements of 2NF and also:

  • There should be no transitive dependencies (a non-key attribute should not depend on another non-key attribute).

Example:

Suppose we have the following table in 2NF:

StudentID Course Instructor Department
1 Math Dr. Smith Math Dept
1 Science Dr. Brown Science Dept
2 Math Dr. Smith Math Dept
2 English Dr. White English Dept
3 Science Dr. Brown Science Dept
3 English Dr. White English Dept

To convert this table to 3NF, we must eliminate transitive dependencies. We can create three tables:

Students and Courses Table:

StudentID Course
1 Math
1 Science
2 Math
2 English
3 Science
3 English

Courses and Instructors Table:

Course Instructor
Math Dr. Smith
Science Dr. Brown
English Dr. White

Instructors and Departments Table:

Instructor Department
Dr. Smith Math Dept
Dr. Brown Science Dept
Dr. White English Dept

Practical Exercises

  1. Exercise 1: Convert to 1NF

    • Given the following table, convert it to 1NF: | OrderID | CustomerName | Products | |---------|--------------|-------------------| | 101 | John Doe | Laptop, Mouse | | 102 | Jane Smith | Keyboard, Monitor |

  2. Exercise 2: Convert to 2NF

    • Given the following table in 1NF, convert it to 2NF: | OrderID | Product | Supplier | SupplierPhone | |---------|----------|----------|---------------| | 101 | Laptop | Dell | 123-456-7890 | | 101 | Mouse | Logitech | 098-765-4321 | | 102 | Keyboard | Logitech | 098-765-4321 | | 102 | Monitor | Samsung | 555-555-5555 |

  3. Exercise 3: Convert to 3NF

    • Given the following table in 2NF, convert it to 3NF: | EmployeeID | EmployeeName | Department | Manager | |------------|--------------|------------|-------------| | 1 | Alice | HR | Bob | | 2 | Charlie | IT | David | | 3 | Eve | HR | Bob | | 4 | Frank | IT | David |

Conclusion

Normalization is a fundamental technique in database design that helps organize data efficiently and avoid redundancy and inconsistency issues. Through normal forms (1NF, 2NF, and 3NF), we can structure our tables to meet these principles. The provided practical exercises will help you apply these concepts and improve your database design skills.
