Introduction

COBOL (Common Business-Oriented Language) is one of the oldest high-level programming languages. It was specifically designed for business, finance, and administrative systems for companies and governments. Understanding the history and evolution of COBOL provides valuable context for its design principles and continued relevance.

Origins of COBOL

  • 1959: COBOL was developed as a result of a conference sponsored by the Department of Defense. The goal was to create a portable programming language for data processing.
  • Committee Formation: The Conference on Data Systems Languages (CODASYL) was formed to guide the development of COBOL.
  • Initial Release: The first COBOL specification was released in 1960.

Key Milestones in COBOL's Evolution

  • 1960s: Early adoption by businesses and government agencies. COBOL programs were primarily used for batch processing.
  • 1974: COBOL-74 standard was released, introducing structured programming constructs.
  • 1985: COBOL-85 standard introduced more modern programming concepts, such as scope terminators and nested subprograms.
  • 2002: COBOL 2002 standard added object-oriented programming features.
  • 2014: COBOL 2014 standard included updates for modern computing environments, such as support for Unicode and XML.

COBOL's Design Principles

  • Business Orientation: COBOL was designed to be readable by non-programmers, particularly business managers.
  • Portability: One of the primary goals was to ensure that COBOL programs could run on different hardware platforms with minimal changes.
  • Self-Documentation: The syntax of COBOL was designed to be self-explanatory, making it easier to understand and maintain.

COBOL Syntax and Structure

COBOL's syntax is verbose and English-like, which makes it unique among programming languages. Here is a simple example of a COBOL program:

IDENTIFICATION DIVISION.
PROGRAM-ID. HelloWorld.

PROCEDURE DIVISION.
    DISPLAY 'Hello, World!'.
    STOP RUN.

Explanation

  • IDENTIFICATION DIVISION: This section contains metadata about the program, such as its name.
  • PROCEDURE DIVISION: This is where the executable code resides. The DISPLAY statement outputs text to the screen, and STOP RUN terminates the program.

COBOL in Modern Context

  • Legacy Systems: Many financial institutions, government agencies, and large corporations still use COBOL for their critical systems.
  • Maintenance and Modernization: There is a significant demand for COBOL programmers to maintain and modernize existing systems.
  • Integration with Modern Technologies: COBOL has evolved to integrate with modern technologies, such as web services and cloud computing.

Conclusion

COBOL has a rich history and has evolved significantly since its inception in 1959. Its design principles of readability, portability, and business orientation have ensured its longevity. Understanding COBOL's history helps appreciate its role in modern computing and the ongoing need for COBOL expertise in maintaining and modernizing legacy systems.
