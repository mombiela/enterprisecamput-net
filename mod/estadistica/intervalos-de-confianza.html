Introduction to Confidence Intervals

Confidence intervals are a fundamental tool in statistics that allow us to estimate a range of values within which a population parameter is expected to lie with a certain level of confidence. This concept is crucial for data-driven decision-making as it provides a measure of the precision and uncertainty associated with estimates.

Key Concepts

  • Population Parameter: A value that describes a characteristic of the entire population (e.g., the population mean).
  • Sample Statistic: A value calculated from a sample used to estimate a population parameter (e.g., the sample mean).
  • Confidence Level: The probability that the confidence interval contains the true population parameter. Commonly used confidence levels are 90%, 95%, and 99%.
  • Standard Error: A measure of the variability of a sample statistic. It is used to calculate the margin of error of the confidence interval.
  • Margin of Error: The amount added and subtracted from the sample statistic to form the confidence interval.

Calculation of Confidence Intervals

To calculate a confidence interval for the population mean when the population standard deviation is known, the following formula is used:

\[ CI = \bar{x} \pm Z \left( \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \right) \]

Where:

  • \(\bar{x}\) is the sample mean.
  • \(Z\) is the critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the desired confidence level.
  • \(\sigma\) is the population standard deviation.
  • \(n\) is the sample size.

Calculation Example

Suppose we have a sample of 100 students and the mean of their exam scores is 75 with a population standard deviation of 10. We want to calculate a 95% confidence interval for the population mean.

  1. Sample mean (\(\bar{x}\)): 75
  2. Population standard deviation (\(\sigma\)): 10
  3. Sample size (\(n\)): 100
  4. Confidence level: 95%
  5. Critical value (\(Z\)): 1.96 (for a 95% confidence level)

Applying the formula:

\[ CI = 75 \pm 1.96 \left( \frac{10}{\sqrt{100}} \right) \] \[ CI = 75 \pm 1.96 \left( 1 \right) \] \[ CI = 75 \pm 1.96 \] \[ CI = [73.04, 76.96] \]

Therefore, the 95% confidence interval for the population mean is [73.04, 76.96].

Confidence Intervals for Proportions

To calculate a confidence interval for a population proportion, the following formula is used:

\[ CI = \hat{p} \pm Z \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p})}{n}} \]

Where:

  • \(\hat{p}\) is the sample proportion.
  • \(Z\) is the critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the desired confidence level.
  • \(n\) is the sample size.

Calculation Example for Proportions

Suppose in a survey of 200 people, 120 said they prefer to work from home. We want to calculate a 95% confidence interval for the population proportion that prefers to work from home.

  1. Sample proportion (\(\hat{p}\)): \(\frac{120}{200} = 0.6\)
  2. Sample size (\(n\)): 200
  3. Confidence level: 95%
  4. Critical value (\(Z\)): 1.96 (for a 95% confidence level)

Applying the formula:

\[ CI = 0.6 \pm 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{0.6(1 - 0.6)}{200}} \] \[ CI = 0.6 \pm 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{0.24}{200}} \] \[ CI = 0.6 \pm 1.96 \sqrt{0.0012} \] \[ CI = 0.6 \pm 1.96 \times 0.0346 \] \[ CI = 0.6 \pm 0.0678 \] \[ CI = [0.5322, 0.6678] \]

Therefore, the 95% confidence interval for the population proportion is [0.5322, 0.6678].

Practical Exercises

  1. Exercise 1: Calculate the 90% confidence interval for the population mean of a sample of 50 people with a sample mean of 80 and a population standard deviation of 15.
  2. Exercise 2: Calculate the 99% confidence interval for the population proportion in a survey of 500 people, where 300 said they prefer to shop online.

Conclusion

Confidence intervals are a powerful tool for estimating population parameters and making informed decisions. Understanding how to calculate and interpret these intervals is essential for any professional working with data. Through examples and practical exercises, we have explored how to apply these concepts in real-world situations, providing a solid foundation for their use in more advanced statistical analyses.
