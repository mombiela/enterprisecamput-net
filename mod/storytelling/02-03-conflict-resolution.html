Conflict and Resolution

Next ►

Conflict and resolution are critical components of storytelling that drive the narrative forward and engage the audience emotionally. Understanding how to effectively incorporate conflict and resolution into your stories can significantly enhance their impact and make them more memorable.

What is Conflict?

Conflict is the central struggle or problem that the characters in a story must face and overcome. It creates tension and suspense, making the story compelling and engaging. Conflict can take various forms, including:

  1. Internal Conflict: A struggle within a character, such as emotional turmoil or moral dilemmas.
  2. External Conflict: A struggle between a character and an outside force, such as another character, society, nature, or technology.

Types of Conflict

Here are some common types of conflict found in storytelling:

Type of Conflict Description Example
Person vs. Self Internal struggle within a character. A character battling addiction or self-doubt.
Person vs. Person Conflict between two characters. A hero facing off against a villain.
Person vs. Society Conflict between a character and societal norms or laws. A protagonist fighting against an unjust system.
Person vs. Nature Conflict between a character and natural forces. A character surviving a natural disaster.
Person vs. Technology Conflict between a character and technological forces or advancements. A character dealing with the consequences of artificial intelligence.

What is Resolution?

Resolution is the part of the story where the conflict is resolved, and the narrative comes to a satisfying conclusion. It provides closure to the story and often includes a moral or lesson learned by the characters.

Elements of Resolution

  1. Climax: The turning point of the story where the conflict reaches its peak.
  2. Falling Action: Events that follow the climax and lead to the resolution.
  3. Denouement: The final outcome of the story, where loose ends are tied up, and the conflict is resolved.

Practical Example

Let's look at a practical example to understand how conflict and resolution work in a story.

Example Story: "The Lost Hiker"

Conflict: A hiker gets lost in a dense forest and must find their way back to safety.

Resolution: The hiker uses their survival skills and determination to navigate through the forest, eventually finding a rescue team and returning home safely.

Breakdown of the Example

  1. Introduction: Introduce the hiker and their background.
  2. Rising Action: The hiker ventures into the forest and realizes they are lost.
  3. Climax: The hiker faces a critical moment where they must decide whether to stay put or continue searching for a way out.
  4. Falling Action: The hiker encounters various challenges but remains determined to find a way out.
  5. Resolution: The hiker successfully finds a rescue team and returns home, having learned valuable lessons about preparation and perseverance.

Exercise: Identifying Conflict and Resolution

Task

Read the following short story and identify the conflict and resolution:

Story: "The Overworked Employee"

Sarah is an employee at a busy marketing firm. She is constantly overwhelmed with work and feels like she can't keep up with the demands of her job. One day, she decides to speak to her manager about her workload. Her manager listens and agrees to redistribute some of her tasks to other team members. Sarah feels relieved and more capable of handling her responsibilities.

Solution

  • Conflict: Sarah is overwhelmed with work and feels unable to keep up with the demands of her job.
  • Resolution: Sarah speaks to her manager, who agrees to redistribute some of her tasks, relieving her stress and making her workload more manageable.

Common Mistakes and Tips

Common Mistakes

  1. Lack of Clear Conflict: Without a clear conflict, the story may lack direction and fail to engage the audience.
  2. Unresolved Conflict: Leaving conflicts unresolved can leave the audience unsatisfied.
  3. Overcomplicating the Resolution: A resolution should be clear and logical, not overly complicated or unrealistic.

Tips

  1. Build Tension Gradually: Gradually build tension to keep the audience engaged.
  2. Ensure Logical Resolution: Make sure the resolution logically follows the events of the story.
  3. Reflect on the Conflict: Use the resolution to reflect on the conflict and highlight the growth or change in the characters.

Conclusion

Conflict and resolution are essential elements of storytelling that create tension and provide closure. By understanding and effectively incorporating these elements, you can craft compelling stories that resonate with your audience.
