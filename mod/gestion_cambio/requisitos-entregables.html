In this section of the course on Change Management, we will address the concepts of requirements and deliverables, fundamental to ensuring the success of any change project in an organization. We will understand what they are, how they are defined and managed, and their importance in the context of change management.

Requirements

Requirements are the conditions or capabilities that must be met or achieved in a project. In the context of change management, requirements can be both technical and non-technical and must be clearly defined and documented.

Types of Requirements

  • Functional: Describe what the system must do.
    • Example: "The system must allow users to log in with a username and password."
  • Non-Functional: Describe how the system should behave.
    • Example: "The system must be able to handle 1000 concurrent users."
  • Business Requirements: Describe the needs of the organization.
    • Example: "The new system must reduce order processing time by 20%."
  • User Requirements: Describe the needs and expectations of end users.
    • Example: "Users must be able to access the system from mobile devices."

Example of a Requirements Document

# Project XYZ Requirements Document

## Functional Requirements
1. The system must allow users to register with an email address and password.
2. The system must send a confirmation email after registration.

## Non-Functional Requirements
1. The system must be accessible 24/7.
2. The system's response time must not exceed 2 seconds.

## Business Requirements
1. The system must integrate with the existing inventory management system.
2. The system must generate monthly sales reports.

## User Requirements
1. Users must be able to recover their password via a link sent to their email.
2. Users must be able to customize their profile.

Deliverables

Deliverables are the products, services, or results that must be completed and delivered during a project. In change management, deliverables can include documents, software, reports, among others.

Types of Deliverables

  • Documentation: User manuals, installation guides, requirements documents.
    • Example: "User manual for the new inventory management system."
  • Software: Applications, modules, updates.
    • Example: "Version 1.0 of the inventory management system."
  • Reports: Impact analysis, progress reports, post-implementation evaluations.
    • Example: "Impact analysis report of the change in the sales process."
  • Training: Training programs, workshops, seminars.
    • Example: "Training workshop for the use of the new inventory management system."

Example of a Deliverables List

# Project XYZ Deliverables List

1. System Requirements Document
2. User Manual
3. Version 1.0 of the Inventory Management System
4. Impact Analysis Report
5. Training Program for End Users

Comparison of Requirements and Deliverables

| Aspect | Requirements | Deliverables | |--------------|---------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------| | Definition | Necessary conditions or capabilities | Products, services, or results to be delivered | | Types | Functional, Non-Functional, Business, User | Documentation, Software, Reports, Training | | Example | "The system must allow login" | "User Manual of the system" | | Purpose | Establish what is needed | Provide what has been developed or produced |

Conclusion

In summary, requirements and deliverables are essential components in change management. Requirements define what is needed for the project to succeed, while deliverables are the tangible results produced throughout the project. A clear understanding and management of both elements are crucial to ensuring that the change is effectively implemented and meets the expectations of all stakeholders.
