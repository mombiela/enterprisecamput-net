Introduction

In this section of the Creativity course, we will explore two fundamental techniques for generating ideas: Brainstorming and Brainwriting. Both methodologies are powerful tools for fostering creativity and innovation in work teams.

Brainstorming

Brainstorming is a group idea generation technique used to find creative solutions to specific problems. It was popularized by Alex Osborn in the 1940s and is based on the premise that the quantity of ideas generated can lead to higher quality solutions.

Principles of Brainstorming

  • Suspension of judgment: During the session, all ideas are welcome and should not be criticized.
  • Quantity over quality: The more ideas generated, the better. Quantity can lead to quality.
  • Combination and improvement: Ideas can be combined and improved upon mutually.
  • Freedom of thought: Participants are encouraged to think outside the box and propose unusual ideas.

Steps for a Brainstorming session

  1. Define the problem: Clarify the problem or the objective of the session.
  2. Form the group: Gather a diverse group of people.
  3. Establish rules: Remind participants of the principles of Brainstorming.
  4. Generate ideas: Allow participants to propose ideas freely.
  5. Record ideas: Write down all ideas without judging them.
  6. Evaluate and select: After the session, review and select the most promising ideas.

Practical example

Suppose a technology company wants to develop a new mobile application. During a Brainstorming session, participants might generate ideas such as:

  • A habit tracking app.
  • A language learning platform.
  • A project management tool.

Brainwriting

Brainwriting is a technique similar to Brainstorming, but it is done in writing. This methodology can be useful for those who prefer to reflect before sharing their ideas and to avoid the domination of the conversation by a few.

Principles of Brainwriting

  • Equal participation: All participants have the same opportunity to contribute.
  • Anonymous and without interruptions: Ideas are written anonymously, which can reduce social pressure.
  • Time to reflect: Participants have time to think before writing their ideas.

Steps for a Brainwriting session

  1. Define the problem: Clarify the problem or the objective of the session.
  2. Distribute sheets of paper: Each participant receives a sheet of paper.
  3. Write ideas: Participants write their ideas within a set time.
  4. Exchange sheets: The sheets are passed to other participants to add more ideas.
  5. Repeat the process: This cycle is repeated several times.
  6. Collect and evaluate: At the end, all sheets are collected and the ideas generated are evaluated.

Practical example

In the same scenario of the technology company, during a Brainwriting session, participants might write ideas such as:

  • A meditation app.
  • A personal data analysis tool.
  • An educational game for children.

Comparison between Brainstorming and Brainwriting

| Aspect | Brainstorming | Brainwriting | |------------------|----------------------------------------|----------------------------------------| | Participation | Verbal and in group | Written and more individual | | Dynamics | Interactive and spontaneous | Structured and reflective | | Social pressure | Can be high | Reduced by anonymity | | Reflection time | Less, quick ideas | More, allows reflection | | Idea recording | Needs a facilitator to write down | Each participant writes their ideas |

Conclusion

Brainstorming and Brainwriting are complementary techniques that can be used to foster creativity in work teams. While Brainstorming is more dynamic and allows for immediate interaction, Brainwriting offers a space for individual reflection and can reduce social pressure. Both methodologies, when applied correctly, can generate a wide range of innovative ideas and effective solutions.
